Learning Objectives

By the end of this sequence you will be able to:

  1. Describe barter, koha, and modern currency as different systems of exchange.
  2. Explain what makes an exchange fair, using evidence from the simulation.
  3. Connect koha, utu, and manaakitanga to exchange practices past and present.
  4. Recognise online threats including phishing, scam websites, and fraudulent listings.
  5. Apply the Look–Check–Pause framework to digital communications.
  6. Know where to get help if you encounter a suspicious digital interaction.

Success Criteria — I can…

  • Explain the difference between barter and koha in my own words.
  • Identify at least one limitation of a barter economy.
  • Explain what utu means and why reciprocity matters in exchange.
  • Connect the idea of trust across barter, koha, and modern money systems.
  • Recognise three or more red flags in a phishing email or scam website.
  • Apply the Look–Check–Pause steps to an unfamiliar digital scenario.
  • Explain what I should do if I receive a suspicious message asking for money or personal information.
  • Name two resources where I can get help if I think I have been scammed.

Sheet 1 of 8 Context Reference

Barter, Koha & Utu

Lesson 1 — Keep this sheet out throughout the lesson as a reference.

Key Concepts

1
Barter
Directly trading goods or services without money. Both sides must want what the other has. E.g. 3 fish for 2 kūmara.
2
Koha
A gift given freely, without expectation of immediate return. Grounded in manaakitanga — showing care, generosity, and respect.
3
Utu
Reciprocity — maintaining balance in relationships over time. Not transactional, but relational. Restoring harmony, not just "paying back."

Vocabulary

Term Meaning
Ākonga Learners / students
Kaimoana Seafood
Kūmara Sweet potato — a taonga food in te ao Māori
Manaakitanga Hospitality, generosity, showing respect and care for others
Tāonga Treasured things — material and intangible (knowledge, relationships, language)
Harakeke Flax — used for weaving, a symbol of whānau
Toki Adze — a traditional carving and working tool

Key concept: Every exchange depends on trust. When trust breaks down, the system breaks down.

The Barter Simulation

Lesson 1 — Work in groups of 4–5. Your teacher will assign your group a resource card.

The Scenario

Your hapū is preparing for a celebration. Each whānau group has a surplus of one resource and a deficit of other resources. Your goal: get what you need through negotiation and trade. You cannot use money.

Rules:

  • You may only trade resource cards you currently hold.
  • All trades must be agreed and finalised with a handshake.
  • Trades are voluntary (no group can be forced to trade).
  • Trades may involve more than two groups.
  • Trades cannot be undone once finalised.

Your Verification Frameworks

Lesson 2 — Keep this out during both activities.

Physical: Look – Feel – Tilt (NZ banknotes)

👁️

Look

  • Dominant colour matches the denomination
  • Correct person, animal, and landmarks on each side
  • Puzzle number: front & back halves align ✦
  • Holographic window present ✦
🖐️

Feel

  • Thick, smooth polymer (not thin or papery)
  • Raised (intaglio) ink on key text & numerals
🔄

Tilt

  • Rolling colour bar moves when tilted ✦
  • Hologram shifts to reveal fine detail ✦
🔍

Hidden

  • Fluorescent (UV) elements under ultraviolet light ✦
  • Microtext readable only under magnification ✦

✦ These features require holding the note to light or tilting (not applicable to a paper printout). RBNZ: How to spot a fake ↗

Digital — Look – Check – Pause

Before you click, buy, or share:

L
Look
Examine what is in front of you.
• Check the sender address or URL carefully; is it the real domain?
• Does the logo, layout, or spelling look slightly wrong?
• Is the price, offer, or request unusual?
C
Check
Verify before you act.
• Go directly to the official website; don't click the link in the message.
• Search the sender's name + “scam” online.
• Call the organisation directly using a number you find yourself.
P
Pause
Don't act in a hurry.
• Urgency (“24 hours!” “Act now!”) is a manipulation tactic.
• Slow down. Tell a trusted adult if unsure.
• If something feels wrong, it probably is.

Compare to the Look–Feel–Tilt test for NZ banknotes. Same principle, different medium. Critical attention protects you in both worlds.

Common Red Flags — quick reference

Type Red flags to look for
Email / Text Generic greeting ("Dear Customer"), wrong sender domain, urgency, unexpected link, requests for personal info
Website URL doesn't match the real organisation, no HTTPS padlock, poor spelling, pressure to enter bank details
Online listing Price far below market value, new/no-rating seller, cash-only or bank transfer only, vague location
Social media Unfamiliar account, "too good to be true" competition, asking you to DM personal details
Remember: Legitimate organisations will never ask for your password, PIN, or full bank account number by email or text. If they do, it is a scam.
